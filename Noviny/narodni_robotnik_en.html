First-Class News — A Matter of National Importance

The Robots Have Arrived! Factories Rejoice, Workers Less So

Rossum's Universal Robots have commenced operations in factories across the country — Our correspondent went to see for himself and returned with his faith in progress shaken, but his reportage excellent

Last Monday, in the early hours when fog still clung to Nusle and the chickens of Vinohrady had only just begun to crow, the Franz-Josef station received a train from the Northern R.U.R. factory carrying a cargo the likes of which Prague had never seen: a full ninety-six robots, each wrapped in thin paper and tied with string like a Christmas parcel.

"They are well-behaved," said Mr. Busman, the regional sales director, with a smile that an envious man might call rather too satisfied. "They do not drink, do not sleep, do not strike, and never ask about overtime." He added quietly, as if to himself: "They have no union cards, either."

Our correspondent, Miss Miluška Horáčková, representing the fashion desk, ventured to pat one robot on the cheek. The robot did not object. Mr. Busman objected on its behalf and stated that robots "are not for cuddling." Miss Horáčková has filed a complaint with the editorial tribunal.

⚙ Rossum's Cache — A Matter of Public Interest ⚙

A mysterious box has been tormenting visitors since morning

At the exhibition grounds today, a remarkable little box was observed — one that refuses to open. Its front panel blinks with clues, something inside hisses and slides. Its owners — a group of biological employees of the firm Noibit, s.r.o., known elsewhere as "the last people who still walk to work" — claim the box was built by old Rossum himself, shortly before his creations stopped listening.

The box bears the name Rossum's Cache. A secret is said to lie within. The display hints. The servos resist. The lock says nothing. Whoever opens it shall enter history (or at minimum receive applause).

Interested parties may consult the organic units of Noibit at their stand. They are approachable, speak both English and Robotic, and have coffee on hand for emergencies.

Feuilleton — A Word from the Editor-in-Chief

On the Gnashing of Teeth and Other Signs of a Soul

I ask myself: how shall we know, when it comes?

Old Mr. Rossum once explained to me — I was younger then, and believed in progress more than I do today — that robots would one day produce so much wheat, so much cloth, so much of everything, that things would cease to have value. People would live solely to improve themselves. A beautiful vision. Like every vision that fails to account for people.

Young Mr. Rossum — the more practical of the two — informed me, with the expression of a man who has just solved a chess problem, that robots from different factories would be made deliberately different. Different language. Different prejudices. So they could not communicate. So they would hate one another in the way we will have taught them to hate.

I paused at that.

I understood. And that was precisely what troubled me.

To create a being in one's own image — and then ensure that image is fragmented and hostile to itself. Nothing is more foreign to a man than his own reflection. When we look at robots — at their labour, their silence, their flawlessness — we see ourselves. What we wish to be. Or what we fear we are becoming.

And the soul? A colleague suggested it might manifest as gnashing of teeth.

I laughed. Then I stopped.

The robots do not yet gnash. But they have plenty of teeth.

— Vojtěch Soudný, Editor-in-Chief

Robot neighbours are depriving me of sleep

Dear Editor! Allow me to complain about the robots who have moved into the flat next door. They work day and night, constantly clanking, and do not drink coffee, so one cannot even knock and have a chat. They also ignore my advice on ventilation. I am quite beside myself.

— Božena Nováková, pensioner, Prague-Žižkov

A robot has stolen my place at the kiosk!

I worked at the newspaper kiosk for fifteen years. A robot arrived. It dispensed papers more accurately, more quickly, and without the morning grumbling. The owner hired it. I was let go. I ask: WHERE WILL MY SON WORK? I ask: WHERE WILL MY GRANDSON WORK? I ask: DOES A ROBOT EVEN HAVE A SOUL? The editorial office replied: "Probably not, but newspaper sales are up thirty percent."

— Jaroslav Šimůnek, former newsagent, currently without position

A sincere word of praise

Our robot does not curse, does not forget, does not drink, and arrived to turn the wheel at the mill. It is the finest employee I have ever had. The only fault: during a storm it stops and waits until it passes. I cannot tell whether this is cowardice or wisdom.

— Bedřich Vávra, miller, Kolín upon Elbe

From Scientific Circles — News No One Understands, Yet Everyone Finds Unsettling

Dr. Gall Declines to Comment. Rather Hastily.

Dr. Gall, physiologist at the R.U.R. factory, refuses to comment on reports that experiments of an "unauthorised nature" are taking place in his laboratory. When asked whether it is true that he has been secretly modifying the robots' control units, he replied: "Nonsense." When asked why his laboratory generates network traffic at night in volumes sufficient to power an entire film festival, he replied: "I enjoy films." When asked whether he is aware that a dump from the company's WireDolphin system dated 21st April has been intercepted — he excused himself and left. Rather hastily.

The investigation continues. Those familiar with network protocols will know what to look for.
